# V66 -- Bumba-meu-boi: o auto que e festa, e fe e e identidade
## Research Dossier for the Maranhao Book Project

---

## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

Bumba-meu-boi is the defining cultural expression of the Brazilian state of Maranhao -- a syncretic folk festival that fuses theater, music, dance, and religious devotion around the symbolic death and resurrection of an ox. Originating in the 18th century among enslaved Africans, indigenous peoples, and poor rural workers, it dramatizes the story of Pai Francisco, who kills his master's prized bull to satisfy his pregnant wife Catirina's craving for its tongue, triggering a collective ritual to resurrect the animal. What began as subaltern resistance -- actively repressed and banned by authorities from the 1860s -- has become Maranhao's most important cultural manifestation, recognized as UNESCO Intangible Cultural Heritage of Humanity in December 2019. Today, approximately 450 groups across the state perform in five distinct musical styles (sotaques), generating an estimated R$250 million annually during the June festival season, which draws 300,000 people to a single parade on Sao Marcal Day. It is a living metaphor for the cycle of life, death, and rebirth -- and for the resilience of Maranhao's people.

## MAIN NARRATIVE

### 1. Origins and History

#### The Syncretic Birth (18th Century)

Bumba-meu-boi emerged in mid-18th-century northeastern Brazil among the most marginalized communities: enslaved Africans, indigenous peoples, and poor rural workers (caboclos). It was born from the intersection of three cultural streams:

- **African traditions**: Enslaved Africans brought rhythmic and percussive musical traditions, communal dance forms, and a worldview centered on cycles of life and death. The zabumba sotaque preserves the strongest African rhythmic identity.
- **Indigenous elements**: Tupinamba and other indigenous influences are reflected in the matraca (wooden clappers), in characters like the caboclo de pena (feathered figure) and the caboclo de fita, and in the role of the paje (shaman) in resurrecting the ox.
- **Portuguese theatrical forms**: The *auto* (folk play) structure derives from Iberian dramatic traditions, particularly pastoral plays and religious theater. The narrative framework -- transgression, punishment, miraculous restoration -- echoes medieval European morality plays.

The context was the *Ciclo do Gado* (Cattle Cycle), when extensive cattle ranching dominated the Maranhao economy and depended on enslaved labor. The ox was both an economic foundation and a potent symbol of wealth and power.

#### The Earliest Records

The earliest known documentary reference dates to July 7, 1829, in the journal *O Farol Maranhense* in Sao Luis. Some scholars place the formation period in the final decades of the 18th century. The earliest documented performance near Recife, Pernambuco, occurred in 1840, but the Maranhao tradition is considered the original matrix.

#### 19th-Century Repression

Throughout the 19th century, bumba-meu-boi was subject to intense police repression. Sao Luis's elites, who styled their city the "Atenas Brasileira" (Brazilian Athens) based on a white, European cultural identity, viewed the festival as barbaric, obscene, and dangerous. The press routinely described brincantes as "savages" engaged in "vagrancy."

**The Seven-Year Ban (1862--1868):** For seven consecutive years, bumba-meu-boi was completely banned from the streets of Sao Luis. Authorities cited public order concerns -- the festivities were accused of generating "disturbances" including armed disputes and deaths. Police required special licenses for any performance, and groups were confined to the periphery or rural areas of the island.

The Festa de Sao Marcal itself originated from this repression: police barred groups from entering the Centro (downtown), so performers gathered at the Areal do Joao Paulo, which became their traditional meeting point. Active discrimination against bumba-meu-boi persisted until approximately the 1950s, when folklorist intellectuals began championing its cultural value.

#### Migration to the Amazon

The Great Drought of 1877 sent Maranhense farm workers to the Amazon as rubber-tappers. They carried bumba-meu-boi with them, where it was enriched by Amazonian music and folklore, eventually becoming the *Boi-Bumba* tradition of Parintins.

### 2. The Auto: The Folk Play Narrative

The *auto do bumba-meu-boi* tells a deceptively simple story with profound social resonance:

**The Plot:** Pai Francisco (also called Negro Chico), a Black cowboy/enslaved man on a cattle ranch, has a pregnant wife, Catirina (Mae Catirina). She develops an intense craving (*desejo*) for the tongue of the farm owner's most prized bull. Fearing for her unborn child's health if the craving goes unsatisfied, Pai Francisco kills the ox. The *Amo* (master/farm owner) discovers the crime and sends cowboys and indigenous trackers to capture Francisco. Various figures attempt to resurrect the bull -- a doctor, a priest -- but all fail. Finally, a *paje* (indigenous shaman) or *cazumba* succeeds in bringing the ox back to life through ritual. The Amo pardons Francisco, and the community celebrates.

**Deeper Reading:** Scholars note that behind the "hero" Francisco, it is Catirina who truly drives the narrative -- her desire initiates the entire cycle. The story encodes colonial power relations: the enslaved man defies the master's property rights, is hunted and threatened, but ultimately pardoned when order (the ox's life) is restored. It is simultaneously a story of resistance and accommodation, of African creativity under Portuguese domination, and of indigenous spiritual power solving what European authority (doctor, priest) cannot.

**Scholarly Debate:** Some researchers, notably those studying the "illusion of archaism" in folklore studies, question whether the *folguedo do boi* originally corresponded to a formal *auto* or whether the fixed narrative was retroactively constructed by 20th-century folklorists from more fluid performative traditions.

**Key Characters:**
- **Pai Francisco (Negro Chico):** The enslaved cowboy; transgressor and everyman hero
- **Catirina (Mae Catirina):** The pregnant wife; traditionally played by a man; provocative, comic, and the true engine of the plot
- **O Amo:** The farm owner; commands the group with whistle and maraca; sings the main *toadas* (songs)
- **Cazumba:** Mystical half-man, half-beast figure with wooden masks and wide robes; exclusive to the sotaque da baixada; performs the witchcraft that resurrects the ox
- **Caboclo de Fita (Rajado):** Wears a hat adorned with 400+ colorful ribbons, hand-embroidered with beadwork; symbolizes the fence protecting cattle; guides and protects the ox in the dance
- **Caboclo de Pena:** Covered in feathers with a large feathered hat (*coca*); represents indigenous warriors in ritual
- **Vaqueiros:** Cowboys who help track Pai Francisco and protect the ox
- **Indios/Indias:** Indigenous characters who participate in the hunt and celebration
- **Burrinha:** A small horse/donkey figure worn by a performer
- **Miolo:** The performer inside the ox structure who animates it

### 3. The Five Sotaques (Musical Styles)

The word *sotaque* means "accent" -- each style is a distinct dialect of bumba-meu-boi, with different instruments, rhythms, costumes, characters, regional origins, and social histories. The five sotaques were codified by brincantes themselves and by academics.

#### Sotaque de Matraca (or Sotaque da Ilha)
- **Origin:** Sao Luis (island of Sao Luis); indigenous (Tupinamba) influence
- **Instrument:** Matraca -- two small pieces of wood clapped together by all participants, creating a massive, thunderous percussive wall
- **Character:** Most popular sotaque; largest number of groups; "batalhoes" (battalions) of up to 1,000 people
- **Key groups:** Boi de Maracana, Boi da Maioba, Boi da Madre Deus, Boi da Pindoba, Boi de Iguaiba, Boi de Ribamar
- **Rivalry:** Boi de Maracana vs. Boi da Maioba -- "the two groups that most divide the hearts of Sao Luis"
- **Note:** Maracana is the most admired by intellectuals; Maioba is the most popular

#### Sotaque de Zabumba (or Sotaque de Guimaraes)
- **Origin:** Municipality of Guimaraes and surrounding region; strongest African influence
- **Instruments:** Zabumbas (large drums), pandeirinhos, maracas, tantas
- **Character:** Considered the *original* rhythm of bumba-meu-boi; slowest, heaviest cadence; the entire bull's head is embroidered
- **Key groups:** Boi de Leonardo, Boi de Vila Passos, Boi da Fe em Deus, Boi Unidos Venceremos, Boi de Guimaraes
- **Crisis:** Facing decline due to lack of new brincantes interested in maintaining the tradition -- the most endangered sotaque

#### Sotaque de Orquestra
- **Origin:** Municipalities of Rosario and Axixa (Munim region); emerged 1950s--1960s
- **Instruments:** Banjos, saxophones, trombones, trumpets, clarinets alongside percussion (maracas, drums)
- **Character:** The youngest sotaque but fastest-growing numerically; fast, melodic rhythm; origins traced to a bordello orchestra in Rosario (1920s--30s)
- **Key groups:** Boi de Axixa (founded 1959, oldest active orquestra group), Boi de Sao Simao, Boi de Nina Rodrigues, Boi de Morros, Boi de Rosario, Boi Brilho da Ilha, Boi Novilho Branco
- **Costumes:** Vests (peitilhos) and skirts of velvet adorned with beads and sequins

#### Sotaque da Baixada (or Sotaque de Pindare)
- **Origin:** Baixada Maranhense -- cities of Sao Joao Batista and Viana
- **Instruments:** Small matracas, pandeiros, caixas, tambores-onca, maracas
- **Character:** The slowest rhythm; lighter, more delicate sound; home of the *Cazumba* character (exclusive to this sotaque)
- **Key groups:** Boi da Floresta de Apolonio, Boi Oriente, Boi Uniao da Baixada, Boi de Pindare, Boi Unidos de Santa Fe, Boi Penalva do Bairro de Fatima
- **Distinctive:** The cazumba wears a wooden mask, a wide embroidered velvet robe (bata), and carries a rattle; beneath the bata, a wooden frame or palm basket widens the hips

#### Sotaque Costa-de-mao (or Sotaque de Cururupu)
- **Origin:** Cururupu region
- **Instrument:** Small pandeiros (tambourines) struck with the *backs of the hands* (costas da mao)
- **Origin story:** According to oral tradition (Mestre Eliesio of Boi Brilho da Sociedade), enslaved people were punished by having their hands beaten. Even with wounded palms, they would not miss the Sao Joao celebration, so they played the pandeiros with the backs of their hands.
- **Crisis:** Only two groups remain; very limited presence in the capital's programming; the most endangered sotaque alongside zabumba

### 4. The Religious Dimension

Bumba-meu-boi is not merely a performance -- it is an act of faith. The religious dimension interweaves Catholic devotion, African spiritual practices, and indigenous cosmology.

#### The June Saints
- **Santo Antonio (June 13):** Marks the beginning of the performance season
- **Sao Joao (June 24):** The patron saint of bumba-meu-boi; the baptism of the ox takes place on the eve (June 23)
- **Sao Pedro (June 29):** Dawn celebration at the Chapel of Sao Pedro in Madre Deus
- **Sao Marcal (June 30):** The grand parade on Avenida Sao Marcal in Joao Paulo; the "crowning" of the June cycle

#### The Promessa (Religious Vow)
Many bumba-meu-boi groups are created and sustained through *promessas* -- religious vows made to the June saints. A family or community promises to maintain a boi group if a prayer is answered (healing from illness, a safe birth, economic relief). Breaking the promise is believed to bring spiritual consequences. This sacred obligation gives the tradition an existential weight beyond entertainment.

#### The Baptism
On the eve of Sao Joao (June 23), the boi is baptized in a ceremony that mirrors Catholic baptism: a priest, rezadeira (prayer woman), or padrinho (godfather) officiates, using holy water. Only after baptism does the boi receive Sao Joao's "permission" to take to the streets. The ceremony carries deep spiritual gravity.

#### The Death of the Ox
Between July and December (varying by group), the boi "dies" in a symbolic ceremony marked by farewell songs, distribution of food, and in some cases, offerings. Wine is distributed representing the ox's blood, symbolically offered to Sao Joao. The death closes the annual cycle and renews the promise for the following year.

#### Syncretism
The tradition interweaves Catholic saints, African-derived spiritual practices (including terreiro rituals and Tambor de Mina connections), and indigenous healing/cosmological elements (the paje's resurrection of the ox). This triple syncretism is fundamental to Maranhao's cultural identity.

### 5. The Major Groups

#### Boi de Maracana (Sotaque de Matraca)
- **Age:** Over 100 years of continuous existence
- **Location:** Maracana community, ~25 km from central Sao Luis, known for jucarara (acai) plantations
- **Size:** ~300 costumed members; ~1,000 total participants during performances
- **Legendary leader:** Humberto de Maracana (Humberto Barbosa Mendes, 1939--2015), known as "Guriata" (songbird). Commanded the boi for 40+ years as amo, principal cantador, and composer. His toada "Maranhao, meu tesouro, meu torrao" became an unofficial state anthem, recorded by singer Alcione. Recognized as Mestre em Cultura Popular by the Ministry of Culture; winner of the 23rd Premio da Musica Brasileira.
- **Current leadership:** Maria Jose (Humberto's successor), a pedagogue and social worker, directs 100+ brincantes. Humberto's sons Ribinha, Teteco, and Humberto Filho continue the musical legacy.
- **Morte do boi:** Second Saturday of August

#### Boi da Maioba (Sotaque de Matraca)
- The most popular group in Sao Luis (while Maracana is the most admired by intellectuals)
- Among the first matraca groups created, alongside Madre Deus and Sitio do Apicum
- The Maracana-Maioba rivalry is the defining cultural competition of Sao Luis's June season

#### Boi Barrica
- **Founded:** 1985, in the Madre Deus neighborhood, by Ze de Franca Pereira
- **Distinctive:** Covers all five sotaques in a single performance, plus cacuria, danca do coco, and quadrilha
- **Size:** Started with ~50 participants; grew to ~150 by 2017

#### Boi da Madre Deus (Sotaque de Matraca)
- One of the founding matraca groups
- Based in the historically significant Madre Deus neighborhood of Sao Luis

#### Boi de Axixa (Sotaque de Orquestra)
- **Founded:** 1959, city of Axixa
- **Distinction:** Oldest active orquestra-style bumba-meu-boi in Maranhao
- **Current leader:** Leila Naiva, daughter of founder Francisco Naiva
- **Size:** ~150 members (brincantes, musicians, embroiderers, cooks, artisans, orchestra)
- **Innovation:** In 2024, debuted "Axixazinho" -- a children's boi of ~30 young performers, ensuring intergenerational transmission

#### Boi de Pindare (Sotaque da Baixada)
- Representative of the Baixada Maranhense tradition
- Noted for its cazumba characters and slower, more delicate rhythm

### 6. Costumes and Visual Art

The visual dimension of bumba-meu-boi is an art form unto itself.

#### The Couro do Boi
The *couro* (literally "leather" or "hide") is the embroidered fabric covering the ox structure -- generally velvet, but sometimes other materials. It is the single most important visual element:
- Takes 3--6 months to complete
- May involve multiple artisans
- Embroidered with canutilho (bugle beads), micanga (small beads), paete (sequins), stones, braids, and trimmings
- Each artisan develops signature techniques for bead placement, outlining, and filling
- In the zabumba style, the bull's entire head and tail are individually embroidered
- Estimated cost: ~R$5,000 for a complete couro with embroidery (2017 prices)

#### Costume Elements
- **Indias:** Costumes made with real feathers (pheasant, swan, chinchilla, duck, rooster tail, peacock); ~R$800 per costume
- **Vaqueiros:** Simpler costumes; ~R$400 each
- **Caboclo de Fita hats:** Hand-embroidered with beadwork and sequins; adorned with 400+ colorful ribbons; large and heavy
- **Cazumba (Baixada style):** Wide embroidered velvet bata (gown), carved wooden mask of varied and sometimes frightening designs, rattle; a palm basket or wooden frame beneath the gown widens the hips
- **Orquestra style:** Vests (peitilhos) and skirts of velvet adorned with beads and sequins
- **Performers (Pai Francisco, Catirina, Miolo):** Less expensive costumes but performers are paid ~R$100/night

#### Artisan Communities
Embroidery is done by hand and by sewing machine, by both women and men, working alone or in family units. In some communities, around 12 artisans dedicate themselves full-time to bumba-meu-boi embroidery and cazumba mask-making. The artisan tradition represents a distinct creative economy.

### 7. The Annual Ritual Cycle

The bumba-meu-boi cycle is organized into four phases:

1. **Ensaios (Rehearsals) -- April/May to mid-June:** Groups prepare new toadas, costumes, and choreographies. New couro is commissioned and embroidered.

2. **Batismo (Baptism) -- June 23 (eve of Sao Joao):** The ox is baptized with holy water, receiving the saint's permission to perform. This is the spiritual authorization for the brincadeira.

3. **Apresentacoes (Performances) -- June 13 through end of June/early July:**
   - June 13 (Santo Antonio): Season begins
   - June 24 (Sao Joao): Core celebration
   - June 29 (Sao Pedro): Dawn celebration at the Chapel of Sao Pedro, Madre Deus
   - June 30 (Sao Marcal): Grand parade on Avenida Sao Marcal, Joao Paulo neighborhood -- the "crowning" of the season
   - Presentations continue through July in arraiais (festival grounds) across the state

4. **Morte do Boi (Death of the Ox) -- July through December:** Each group schedules its own morte. Farewell songs, distribution of food, offering of wine (symbolizing the ox's blood). The death closes the cycle and renews the sacred pact with Sao Joao.

#### The Festa de Sao Marcal
The single most spectacular event of the season:
- First documented encounter: June 29, 1928, when battalions gathered in what is now Praca Ivar Saldanha
- The street was officially renamed from Avenida Joao Pessoa to Avenida Sao Marcal
- Draws ~300,000 spectators and 25--36 matraca groups
- Begins ~6:00 AM, ends when the last group finishes (~2:00 AM the next day)
- June 30 is also the Dia Nacional do Bumba Meu Boi (National Day of Bumba Meu Boi)
- ~500 police officers deployed for security

### 8. UNESCO Recognition (2019)

#### The Path to Inscription
- **2011:** IPHAN registers Bumba-meu-boi as Patrimonio Cultural Imaterial do Brasil
- **2012:** Application filed for UNESCO's Representative List
- **2019, December 11:** Unanimously inscribed with honors at the 14th session of the Intergovernmental Committee (14.COM) in Bogota, Colombia

#### Criteria and Process
The nomination followed the 2003 UNESCO Convention for the Safeguarding of the Intangible Cultural Heritage. Key elements of the dossier:
- Demonstrated cultural exceptionality
- Community-driven nomination (communities first proposed inclusion to IPHAN)
- Evaluated by the Chamber of Intangible Heritage and Advisory Council of Cultural Heritage
- Required elaboration of a Safeguard Plan covering identification, documentation, preservation, promotion, protection, and valorization

#### What Made Maranhao's Tradition Distinctive
While similar ox-traditions exist across Brazil, Maranhao's was recognized specifically for:
- The intrinsic relationship between faith, festivities, and art
- The diversity of five distinct sotaques
- The annual reinvention (new songs, new embroideries, new comedies each year)
- The metaphorical reproduction of the cycle of life, death, and rebirth
- The practitioners' relationship with the sacred, mediated by the figure of the ox

#### Impact
Bumba-meu-boi became the 6th Brazilian element on the UNESCO intangible heritage list, joining Arte Kusiwa (2003), Samba de Roda (2005), Frevo (2012), Cirio de Nossa Senhora de Nazare (2013), and Roda de Capoeira (2014).

IPHAN committed to strengthening groups' autonomy, expanding heritage education, performing new documentation, and enhancing research.

### 9. Economic and Social Dimensions

#### Scale
- **~450 groups/communities** across Maranhao (2025 figure; up from ~300 estimated in 2017)
- **100+ groups** on the island of Sao Luis alone (mapped by the UFMA "Caminhos da Boiada" project)
- Groups range from ~50 members (small bois) to 300+ costumed performers and 1,000+ total participants

#### Economic Impact
- The 2024 festas juninas generated approximately **R$250 million** (~US$50 million) in Sao Luis's economy
- The state government invested **R$53 million** in 2024
- In June 2025, **89,657 arrivals** were recorded at Sao Luis's international airport -- highest in 13 years, a 19.03% increase over 2024
- The festival season spans 65 days (May previews through July), with 1,000+ scheduled performances
- Sectors boosted: tourism, commerce, hospitality, services, creative economy (artisans, musicians, embroiderers)

#### Social Significance
- **38%** of Sao Luis residents actively participate in June celebrations (highest rate among all Brazilian state capitals, 10 points above national average)
- **53%** of Sao Luis's population considers festas juninas the most important cultural event (vs. 10% for Carnival)
- Sao Luis has been officially recognized as the **capital brasileira das festas juninas** (Brazilian capital of June festivals)
- Groups have performed internationally, transcending local and national barriers

#### The Rivalry
The Maracana vs. Maioba competition in the matraca sotaque is the defining cultural rivalry, comparable to football club loyalties. Families and neighborhoods identify with one boi, and the competition drives creativity, investment, and participation.

### 10. Bumba-meu-boi vs. Boi-Bumba de Parintins

| Dimension | Bumba-meu-boi (Maranhao) | Boi-Bumba (Parintins, Amazonas) |
|---|---|---|
| **Format** | Devotional celebration; no formal competition | Competitive spectacle between two teams |
| **Number of groups** | ~450 across the state | 2 (Garantido and Caprichoso) |
| **Performance space** | Streets, arraiais, terreiros (dispersed) | Bumbodromo arena (centralized) |
| **Musical styles** | 5 distinct sotaques | Unified toada style |
| **Religious dimension** | Central; promessas, baptism, morte do boi | Present but secondary to spectacle |
| **Scale** | Community-based, grassroots | Grand production (floats, comparable to Carnival samba schools) |
| **Resurrection agent** | Cazumba, paje, or various characters | Paje (indigenous shaman) |
| **Heritage status** | UNESCO Intangible Heritage (2019) | IPHAN National Heritage (2018) |
| **Historical relationship** | Original matrix (18th century) | Derived tradition (post-1877 drought migration) |

### 11. Key Figures

#### Humberto de Maracana (1939--2015)
The most iconic figure in bumba-meu-boi history. Born November 2, 1939, in Sao Luis; died January 19, 2015. Known as "Guriata" (songbird). Amo, principal cantador, and composer of Boi de Maracana for 40+ years. His toada "Maranhao, meu tesouro, meu torrao" was recorded by Alcione and became an unofficial anthem. Recognized as Mestre em Cultura Popular by the Ministry of Culture. Awarded the 23rd Premio da Musica Brasileira. Also a sambista, but dedicated his life to bumba-boi.

#### Maria Jose
Successor to Humberto at Boi de Maracana. Pedagogue and social worker; school administrator in the community. Directs 100+ brincantes and organizes social/cultural projects.

#### Leila Naiva
Daughter of founder Francisco Naiva. Current leader of Boi de Axixa, the oldest active orquestra-style group. Creator of "Axixazinho," the children's boi.

#### Ze de Franca Pereira
Founder of Boi Barrica (1985), the innovative group that performs all five sotaques in a single presentation.

#### Honored Mestres (over 80 years old)
The Maranhao State Legislature held a solemn session honoring elder cantadores including Mestre Tunico, Mestre Valdete Cabeca Branca, Mestre Ze Olhinho, Mestre Ciriaco, Mestre Ze Miguel, and Mestre Sabia, among others.

#### Roberto Ricci
Famous composer and popular instrumentalist from Maranhao; long-time cantador of Boi de Maracana who has described Sao Marcal as "the crowning of our faith, our tradition, and the resistance of the communities."

## SURPRISING FACTS

1. **The origin of "costa-de-mao" is a story of defiance through pain.** According to oral tradition, enslaved people whose palms were beaten as punishment refused to miss the Sao Joao celebration. They played their pandeiros with the backs of their wounded hands -- and the sotaque was born from this act of resistance.

2. **The street that changed its name.** The tradition of the Sao Marcal parade was so powerful that the avenue where groups gathered -- originally named Avenida Joao Pessoa -- was officially renamed Avenida Sao Marcal.

3. **Bumba-meu-boi beats Carnival in its home state.** In Sao Luis, 53% of residents consider the June festivals the most important cultural event; only 10% say Carnival. This is a stark inversion of the national Brazilian stereotype.

4. **The sotaque de orquestra traces back to a brothel band.** Researcher Lady Selma Ferreira Albernaz found that the orquestra style originated from a bordello orchestra in Rosario that would play through the streets in the 1920s--30s.

5. **A single caboclo de fita hat has 400+ ribbons.** Each ribbon symbolizes a stick of the fence protecting cattle. The hats are hand-embroidered with beadwork and sequins and are described as "large and heavy."

6. **From criminal ban to UNESCO honors -- in 157 years.** Bumba-meu-boi was banned from Sao Luis streets in 1862; by 2019 it was unanimously inscribed as UNESCO Intangible Cultural Heritage "with honors."

7. **The zabumba sotaque is dying.** The oldest, most "original" style of bumba-meu-boi -- with the strongest African identity -- is in crisis due to lack of new brincantes. It faces the real possibility of extinction.

8. **Catirina, not Pai Francisco, truly drives the narrative.** Researchers emphasize that behind the apparent hero's journey of Francisco, it is Mae Catirina's desire that initiates the entire cycle. The pregnant woman's craving is the engine of death, resurrection, and renewal.

9. **The morte do boi involves ritual wine.** At the symbolic death of the ox, wine is distributed to participants representing the ox's blood, offered to Sao Joao -- a striking parallel to Catholic Eucharistic symbolism.

10. **Boi Barrica defies tradition by embracing all of it.** Founded in 1985, this group performs all five sotaques in a single presentation, along with cacuria, danca do coco, and quadrilha -- a living encyclopedia of Maranhao's musical heritage.

## RESEARCH GAPS

1. **Precise economic data on the artisan supply chain.** While costume costs from 2017 are documented (R$5,000 for couro, R$800 per india), updated figures and data on the total creative economy (number of artisans employed, annual revenue, supply chain for beads/sequins/velvet/feathers) are scarce.

2. **Detailed demographic data on brincantes.** Age distribution, gender balance, socioeconomic profile of performers across the 450 groups. The UFMA "Caminhos da Boiada" project (2024--2027) may fill this gap.

3. **The zabumba and costa-de-mao crisis.** Limited documentation on why these sotaques are declining and what safeguard measures exist. Only two costa-de-mao groups remain -- their stories deserve urgent recording.

4. **Impact of COVID-19 (2020--2021).** The Sao Marcal festival was canceled in 2020. The broader impact on groups, artisans, and the tradition's continuity during the pandemic is underexplored.

5. **Post-UNESCO inscription effects.** Whether the 2019 inscription has materially changed funding, group formation rates, tourism patterns, or community dynamics. Early evidence (rising airport arrivals, growing state investment) suggests positive trends, but systematic analysis is lacking.

6. **The role of women.** Beyond Maria Jose at Maracana and Leila Naiva at Axixa, the broader role of women as embroiderers, rezadeiras, organizers, and emerging cantadoras deserves fuller documentation.

7. **Connections to Afro-Brazilian religions.** The syncretism with Tambor de Mina, Candomble, and terreiro practices is acknowledged but rarely explored in accessible sources.

8. **Interior groups vs. capital groups.** Most documentation focuses on Sao Luis. The traditions of the 350+ groups in the interior -- their specific histories, economic conditions, and artistic innovations -- remain largely unresearched.

9. **The "auto" debate.** The scholarly argument about whether the folk play narrative was always central to bumba-meu-boi or was retroactively constructed by 20th-century folklorists deserves fuller treatment for a general audience.

10. **Comparison with other Brazilian ox traditions.** Beyond Parintins, systematic comparison with Boi-Mamao (Santa Catarina), Boi Pintadinho (Rio de Janeiro), Boi Calemba (Rio Grande do Norte), and other regional variants.
